What unites these systems is the access template. The user arrives with a query, receives a ranked article list, optionally facets the result by date, year or source, and then reads articles individually. Düring et al. (2024) characterise the Impresso interface as one of "transparent generosity" about exposing intermediate structure; entity links and OCR confidence become visible to the user, who is still expected to come with a search term already in mind. The presupposition works well for most historical research, where the historian arrives with a question already framed. Yet this presupposition falters when the goal is to understand a corpus too vast for article-level reading. The problem deepens in another case: when the historian's answer takes the form of a shape moving across days, such as the rhythm of strike coverage or the absence of a particular voice from print.
